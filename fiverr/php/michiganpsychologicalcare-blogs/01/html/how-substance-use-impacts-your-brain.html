How Substance Use Impacts Your Brain- National Drug and Alcohol Facts Week (January 21-28)

January 21 through the 28th has been deemed National Drug and Alcohol Facts Week. This week can be an incredibly important week to spread facts about the effects of drugs and alcohol on the brain. Why? Because the more one knows about how drugs and alcohol can impact their cognitive and mental health, the more informed decisions they can make regarding substance use.  

How Substance Use Impacts Your Brain

There are three main areas of the brain that are impacted by drugs and alcohol:

  • The brain stem
  • The limbic system
  • The cerebral cortex

As the substance makes its way through the brain, the normal operating systems are disrupted and changed. These changes can drastically impact the neurons and brain circuits- and can have lasting effects that stick around long after the substance is out of one’s system.

How Do Substances Impact the Brain’s Functioning?

As mentioned, substance use can alter the way a person’s brain works. The effect we feel when using substances is not one that comes without a price. This is because that “high” we feel when consuming drugs or alcohol is caused by the substance activating, and often times overloading, the brain’s reward systems, like sending a shot of the neurotransmitter dopamine through our systems.

What is Dopamine?

Dopamine is a neurotransmitter produced by the body that plays a significant role in how we experience pleasure and happiness. The neurotransmitter is also a key player in the brain’s reward system, rewarding us with feelings of pleasure in order to reinforce specific behaviors.

Drugs and Dopamine

As stated, dopamine is activated when one behaves in a manner the body considers to be beneficial. It allows us to feel a sense of pleasure in order to reinforce good behaviors. Substance use, however, can allow drugs or alcohol to take over the brain’s reward system. As the substances travel through the body, they can cause massive amounts of dopamine to release into the system, which creates that sense of “high.” That feeling can quickly become addictive and impact the brain’s ability to create and spread dopamine on its own, building a dependency on the substance.
